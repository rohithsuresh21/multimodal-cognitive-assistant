Student Document Assistant
Domain:AI / ML
Difficulty:Beginner
Team Size:2–4 members
1. Background
Students often struggle to navigate long policy documents, FAQs, notices, and process guides for
academic or administrative tasks. They usually need concise answers and relevant references,
but the source material is spread across multiple documents and is tedious to read end-to-end.
This problem focuses on building a practical GenAI assistant over a bounded document col-
lection. The scope is still approachable for beginner teams, but the final product should do
retrieval and grounded answer generation well, with clear citations and a usable interface.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Design and implement a retrieval-augmented GenAI assistant for a focused
domain such as student policies, hostel rules, admissions FAQs, placement guidelines, or schol-
arship information. Given a user query, the system should retrieve the right source material
and generate a grounded answer with citations.
3. Scope of Work
3.1 Knowledge Base and Preprocessing
•Build a bounded knowledge base from PDFs, webpages, handbook sections, circulars, forms,
or FAQ documents relevant to one focused domain.
•Clean, chunk, normalize, and document the ingestion workflow.
•Organize the source material so that answers can be traced back to specific documents or
sections.
3.2 Core GenAI Pipeline
•Build a retrieval-backed generation pipeline using keyword search, embeddings, reranking,
or other lightweight retrieval techniques.

rial.
•Include at least one trust-building mechanism such as source citations, retrieved context

3.3 Interactive Product Surface
•Provide a simple interface where a user can ask questions, inspect cited sources, and receive
grounded answers.
•Allow users to compare retrieved passages with the final generated answer.
•Teams may optionally add query suggestions, multi-turn follow-ups, or document upload for

4. Technical Constraints
•The full workflow must be reproducible on modest hardware or clearly document any cloud
usage.
•Prompting, retrieval, and generation choices must be explained with respect to quality,

Inventory Planning Assistant
Domain:AI / ML
Difficulty:Beginner
Team Size:2–4 members
1. Background
Small businesses and operations teams need to decide not just what demand will be, but what
to buy, when to reorder, how much safety stock to keep, and how to respond when uncertainty
is high. A raw demand forecast is useful, but the real product value comes from turning that
forecast into operational decisions under real constraints such as storage limits, supplier delays,
or limited budgets.
This problem focuses on building a lightweight inventory-planning assistant that combines prob-
abilistic forecasting with actionable decision support. The scope remains accessible to beginner
teams, but the final product should feel like a usable planning tool rather than a modeling
dashboard alone.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Design and implement an inventory-planning assistant for a simple business
scenario, such as a store, cafeteria, warehouse, or pharmacy. The system must forecast future
demand, estimate uncertainty, and convert those predictions into planning actions such as
reorder timing, purchase quantities, stock-risk alerts, or safety-stock suggestions under practical
operating constraints.
3. Scope of Work
3.1 Problem Selection and Data
•Use an appropriate time-series or tabular demand dataset, such as store sales, product
movement, food demand, or order-volume records.
•Prepare the dataset carefully and explain feature selection, seasonality assumptions, and
possible sources of noise.
•Define the downstream planning task clearly, for example reorder decisions, stockout pre-

3.2 Probabilistic Modeling
•Implement one or more Bayesian or probabilistic forecasting approaches such as Bayesian re-

•Explain priors, likelihoods, posterior reasoning, or approximation methods where relevant.
•Compare the probabilistic approach against at least one simpler baseline forecast.

Graphitti: Graph-Native Web Intelligence
Domain:AI / ML
Difficulty:Intermediate
Team Size:4–6 members
1. Background
Most AI systems interact with the web through flat keyword search or stateless RAG over doc-
ument chunks - with no awareness of the relational structure connecting pages, entities, claims,
and data across sites. Real-world queries require reasoning across multiple hops, domains, and
versions. This problem asks teams to build a system that crawls the open web on demand,
constructs a live queryable knowledge graph via a custom NLP pipeline, and places a multi-
agent reasoning and retrieval layer on top - treating the web as a structured graph, not a bag
of documents.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Design and implement a graph-based ML system that, given a user query
about any public website or domain, crawls and ingests relevant web content into a live het-
erogeneous knowledge graph, routes the query to the most appropriate retrieval strategy, and
answers complex multi-hop questions grounded in the constructed graph with cited provenance.
The system must go substantially beyond retrieval-augmented chat. Teams are expected to
design a graph construction pipeline driven by a custom NLP stack, a multi-strategy retrieval
engine with comparative benchmarking, and a genuine agentic orchestrator capable of dynamic
re-planning. An illustrative direction: a co-pilot over developer documentation portals that
answers cross-version queries - reasoning over a version-aware knowledge graph, surfacing con-
tradictions across pages rather than silently merging them.
3. Scope of Work
3.1 Live Crawling and Augmented Dataset Construction
Crawl public pages on demand; follow links to configurable depth; extract structured content
from DOM and metadata. Scraping APIs and crawler agents for cleaning are permitted. Run

a custom NLP pipeline - entity recognition, relation extraction, coreference resolution, seman-
tic chunking, and claim extraction with source attribution. Construct a typed heterogeneous
knowledge graph; node and edge types are a team design decision. Assign confidence scores,
source attribution, and temporal metadata to every edge. Publish the augmented dataset as a
citable, versioned artifact - an original contribution.
3.2 Knowledge Graph Schema and Consistency Agent
Design a typed graph schema; run a consistency agent continuously during ingestion. Detect
conflicting claims, broken references, and schema violations; store conflicts as contested edges
visible to downstream agents - not silently resolved. Design your own conflict representation,
resolution protocol, and rollback mechanism.
3.3 NLP Pipeline: Web Content Graph Triples
Semantic chunking with boundary detection - not fixed-window splitting. Named entity recog-
nition, entity linking, relation extraction (typed subject-predicate-object triples), and corefer-
ence resolution across pages within a crawl session. Claim extraction with source attribution;
context-scoped labeling where applicable. Pipeline evaluated independently on a manually la-
beled held-out set.
3.4 Query Understanding and Routing
Classify incoming natural-language queries by intent before dispatching retrieval. Routing must
be transparent, logged, and ablated - demonstrate it improves over any single fixed strategy.
Explore: intent classification, query rephrasing, multi-hop decomposition - design choices are
yours.
3.5 Multi-Strategy Retrieval Engine
Implement and benchmark at least three distinct retrieval strategies - e.g. dense vector,
sparse/BM25, graph traversal, hybrid fusion, entity-centric (team’s choice). Each strategy
benchmarked independently: precision, recall, and latency on a labeled test set. Final system
must show routing outperforms any single strategy in isolation.
3.6 Multi-Agent Orchestration
Multi-agent architecture with dynamic re-planning when agents return low-confidence results or
hit dead ends. Shared typed working memory (scratchpad); orchestrator guarantees termination
within a configurable latency ceiling. Functional coverage required: crawling, NLP execution,
retrieval selection and execution, answer synthesis with provenance, faithfulness checking, and
background graph integrity monitoring. Agent decomposition is a design decision.
4. Technical Constraints
•NLP pipeline and graph construction must be built by the team; scraping APIs permitted.
•Live graph visualization required in the demo.
•All retrieval strategies must be ablated - report individual performance before combining;
justify why each strategy was chosen.
•Every design decision (graph schema, agent decomposition, retrieval mix) must be justified
with measured impact, not intuition.
•Graph spin-up time (first crawl first successful query) is a primary reported metric.

SPECTRUM
Domain:AI / ML
Difficulty:Intermediate
Team Size:4–6 members
1. Background
While fine-tuning Large Language Models (LLMs) is standard practice, the exact behavioral,
memory, and latency impacts of combining multiple optimization techniques remain poorly un-
derstood. Most existing benchmarks evaluate Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), quan-
tization, pruning, distillation, and alignment in complete isolation, ignoring their compound
interactions.
This problem statement requires teams to close this empirical gap. Teams will conduct rigorous,
systematic research mapping how these methodologies interact when applied sequentially or
concurrently, culminating in a highly optimized, compressed chatbot application.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Select one open-weight LLM family from Hugging Face (e.g., Qwen2.5,
Llama 3, Mistral, Gemma), analyze its underlying architecture, and evaluate the full spectrum
of fine-tuning and compression configurations.
The primary research mandate is to compress a large variant of the chosen family (e.g., Qwen2.5-
14B) down to the size of its official smaller sibling (e.g., Qwen2.5-7B or 3B) using pruning and
knowledge distillation. Teams will then evaluate this custom-compressed model directly against
the native open-source small model to answer a core empirical question:Does a compressed
large model outperform a natively trained small model of matched size?
3. Scope of Work
3.1 Architecture Study & Baseline Profiling
Commit to a single model family and analyze its foundational parameters before initiating any
training workflows:
•Profile attention grouping, layer sensitivity, activation magnitudes, and gradient norms

•Run full fine-tuning to establish an absolute performance ceiling baseline, ablating learning

capabilities, exploring output logits (KL divergence, JSD), intermediate hidden states (MSE,
CKA alignment), and token- or sequence-level objectives.

•Graph and quantify the specific tension between base model capabilities and downstream

•Directly benchmark a minimum of two distinct alignment objectives to justify the final

general knowledge retention at every training milestone.
•Construct a detailed interaction matrix logging benchmark scores, VRAM allocation, and
processing latency for every tested permutation of PEFT, compression, alignment, and dis-
tillation.
•Report median and P95 inference latency under uniform batch sizes, stating exact hardware
configurations.

Domain:AI / ML

1. Background
Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) like LLaVA and Qwen2-VL are increasingly
utilized across fields such as digital forensics, media analysis, education, and intelligent search.
However, establishing user trust remains a challenge because these systems hide their internal
reasoning traces, exposing only final answers.
In multimodal environments, models frequently rely on incorrect image regions, ignore vital
video frames, over-depend on prompt phrasing, or hallucinate visual details entirely. As AI-
generated media grows more pervasive, interpretability and mechanistic forensic auditing of
multimodal AI reasoning have emerged as critical challenges.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Design and implement an interactive platform that audits, visualizes, and
explains the internal reasoning behavior of open-source MLLMs.
The platform must expose hidden interpretability signals to clarify why a model reached a
specific conclusion and evaluate the mathematical stability of that reasoning path. Teams
must develop an end-to-end workflow integrating multimodal inference pipelines, explainability
frameworks, visual attribution checking, and an interactive dashboard interface.

Autonomous Financial Due Diligence Platform
Domain:AI / ML
Difficulty:Intermediate
Team Size:4–6 members
1. Background
Financial due diligence is a highly critical, manually intensive process in private equity, venture
capital, and mergers and acquisitions. Investment analysts routinely spend weeks reviewing
hundreds of financial statements, SEC filings, data rooms, and market updates to identify
risk factors. This reliance on human review makes the process expensive, slow, and prone to
oversight.
Modern large language models matched with multi-agent orchestration can transform this work-
flow. By synthesizing document processing, cross-period financial reasoning, and automated web
information gathering, an intelligent system can compress research timelines from weeks into
hours while surfacing deeper, more uniform risk signals.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:Design and implement an autonomous financial due diligence platform driven
by a coordinated multi-agent team. Given an assortment of public or private target company
data, the system must:
(a) Parse, structure, and normalize erratic unstructured file inputs (PDFs, spreadsheets, SEC
records).
(b) Compute fundamental financial ratios and key performance indicators cleanly without math-
ematical hallucination.
(c) Execute programmatic market intelligence lookups using external application interfaces.
(d) Produce an investment recommendation memo built to professional analyst standards.

Domain:AI / ML

1. Background
When adapting language models to highly specialized professional domains, systems face severe
performance trade-offs. Optimizing a model for quantitative finance might degrade its software
engineering capabilities, and vice versa. Integrating a sparse Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) archi-
tecture with low-rank adapters allows a system to dynamically route different domain tasks to
independent expert modules.
Standard parallel multi-expert methods, however, introduce massive parameter redundancy,
particularly in the down-projection matrices where experts learn near-identical baseline fea-
tures. By executing a targeted architectural restructuring known as MALORA (Mixture of
Asymmetric Low-Rank Adaptation), teams can isolate these overlapping representations into
a frozen, shared low-rank subspace while widening task-specific up-projection experts. This
enables the production of highly specialized, multi-task AI agents optimized for secure, local,
air-gapped deployment.
2. Problem Statement
Core Objective:The objective is to design, implement, and validate a secure, Local AI
Workspace by applying structural surgery to an open-weight dense model family (e.g., Qwen2.5-
7B or Llama-3-8B).
All teams must build the underlying MALORA MoE tensor engine from scratch. However,
teams must choose ONE specialized domain track to build their final product surface around.
The final deliverable must be an end-to-end local desktop software application featuring an
optimized inference server, a track-specific interactive workspace, and a live diagnostic telemetry
dashboard tracking token throughput and active expert routing paths.
