3. Problem Statement
Most AI systems built around mental health operate on a simple classification premise:
given some data about a person, decide whether they are depressed or not. This framing
is clean and easy to evaluate, but it misrepresents how mental health actually works.
Depression, burnout, and anxiety do not appear overnight as binary switches. They
develop gradually, often over weeks or months, and they look different in every person.

A model trained on population-level statistics learns what mental illness looks like on
average — which is not particularly useful for catching it early in any specific individual.
One person’s tense writing is another’s natural style. These ambiguities make population-
level thresholds unreliable for early detection, and they are precisely why current clinical
screening tools like PHQ-9 are periodic and reactive rather than continuous and proactive.
What is actually needed is a system that learns the user first: their normal writing pace,
their usual sentence structure, how their language shifts across moods and days. Only
once that individual baseline is well-established can any deviation from it mean something.
This is the core motivation behind thePersonalized Mental Health Digital Twin—
not to replace clinical judgment, but to offer a continuous, personalized layer of behavioral
monitoring that can flag something worth paying attention to well before it becomes a
crisis.

5. Dataset Plan
5.1 Primary Dataset — DAIC-WOZ
The Distress Analysis Interview Corpus (DAIC-WOZ) is the most suitable publicly available
dataset for this work. It contains clinical interviews with participants across a range of
PHQ-8 depression scores, with accompanying transcripts and self-reported mood labels
that can serve as proxy supervision for the risk prediction module. The data is longitudinal
enough to construct short multi-session trajectories per participant, which is exactly the
temporal structure our model needs.
We supplement this with the CLPsych shared task datasets, which provide annotated
social media text from Reddit and Twitter with mental health labels. These are useful
for pre-training the feature extraction components, even though the domain differs from
journaling.
5.2 The Cold-Start Problem and Synthetic Augmentation
Neitherdatasetprovideswhatthesystemultimatelyneedsinproduction: dense, day-by-day
journal entries from the same person over weeks. To handle this gap during development
and testing, we will build a synthetic data generator that simulates realistic longitudinal
journal sequences. The generator works by sampling a base behavioral profile (writing
speed, average length, sentiment distribution) and then injecting controlled perturbations.
This lets us test the anomaly detection and baseline modules against sequences where we
know exactly when and how the deviation happens — which would be impossible with
real data.
6. Module Descriptions
Module 1: Data Ingestion & Feature Extraction.Users submit daily text entries
through a lightweight web interface. Each submission is timestamped and stored locally
in encrypted form. From every entry we extract three layers of features: surface linguistic
statistics (type-token ratio, average sentence length, VADER sentiment), semantic em-
beddings via all-MiniLM-L6-v2, and behavioral signals like entry length over time and
submission regularity. Everything is normalized per user before being passed downstream.
Module 2: Personalized Baseline Profiler.After roughly ten entries, the system
builds a per-user statistical profile: a rolling mean and standard deviation across all
feature dimensions, updated via exponentially weighted moving average. Two windows
are maintained — 7-day and 30-day — because comparing them lets us tell apart a rough
week from a sustained trend. The baseline is intentionally simple: Gaussian statistics are
transparent, auditable, and degrade smoothly when data is sparse.
Module 3: Temporal Mental State Encoder.The sequence of daily feature vectors
forms a multivariate time series treated as the user’s mental state trajectory. A Temporal
Fusion Transformer encodes this into a sequence of compact latent vectors — one per
day — capturing temporal dependencies that point-in-time statistics would miss. If data

We apply a Cumulative Sum (CUSUM) filter over these values. This allows the tracking

internally and only surfaced to the dashboard once thresholds clear for two consecutive

Module 5: Risk Prediction & Explainability Engine.An XGBoost classifier
over a rolling window of anomaly signals and aggregated features produces a three-level
risk estimate (low / moderate / elevated) for the coming week. XGBoost was chosen
deliberately over a recurrent model because it is far easier to explain with SHAP values,
and explainability is non-negotiable in anything touching clinical mental health. SHAP
feature importances are mapped to templated natural-language summaries — grounded,
auditable outputs rather than free-form generation.

8. Ethical Considerations & Privacy
• No raw text stored or transmitted.Journal entries are processed locally and
immediately converted to feature vectors. The raw text is never written to any persistent
storage and never leaves the user’s device. What is retained is the derived feature
representation, not the words themselves.
• On-device, encrypted processing.All computation happens locally. The SQLite
database is encrypted via SQLCipher. There is no cloud endpoint, no third-party data
processor, and no network transmission in the prototype.
• Explicit, granular consent.Users opt in to each data modality separately. On-
boarding explains in plain language what is being tracked, how it is used, and how to
delete it.
• The system does not diagnose.Every risk output is framed as a change in
behavioral patterns relative to the user’s own baseline — not a clinical assessment.
Every screen that shows a risk signal includes a visible disclaimer that this is not a
substitute for professional support. We are building a monitoring tool, not a diagnostic
one.

10. Expected Outcomes & Extensions
The immediate goal is a working prototype — something a user can actually interact
with over a period of days and get meaningful, personalized feedback from. Beyond the
prototype:
• Clean, reproducible codebasewith explicit module boundaries that make it straight-
forward for future contributors to swap in a better temporal encoder or a different
anomaly detection method.
• Final report written to conference paper standards,documenting the system
design, experimental results on DAIC-WOZ, and an honest account of limitations. The
work is novel enough — particularly the dual-baseline design and the personalized
thresholding approach — that a workshop paper submission is a realistic target.
• A foundation for something larger.Personalized behavioral monitoring at this
level of granularity does not yet exist as an accessible, open-source tool. The modular
architecture is designed with extension in mind: adding speech or wearable inputs,
plugging in a reinforcement learning layer for adaptive interventions, or integrating
with a mobile platform are all natural next steps that build directly on what we are
constructing here.
