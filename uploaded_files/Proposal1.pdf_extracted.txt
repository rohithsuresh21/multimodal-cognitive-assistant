3. Problem Understanding
Mental health conditions develop gradually and vary significantly across individuals, making
early detection and timely intervention a major challenge. Traditional AI systems rely on static
binary labels (e.g.,depressed / not depressed) that fail to capture the continuous, deeply personal
nature of human cognition and behavior.

This project aims to develop aPersonalized Mental Health Digital Twin (PMHDT)—
an AI-driven virtual representation of an individual’s mental state that:
•Continuously learns from the user’s behavioral and linguistic data.
•Establishes a personalized baseline rather than relying on population-level norms.
•Detects subtle deviations from that baseline as early warning signals.
•Predicts future mental health risks using longitudinal data.
•Delivers interpretable, trust-building explanations for users and clinicians.
4. Proposed Solution
Our solution is structured around five tightly integrated modules that together constitute the
digital twin pipeline.
4.1 Module 1 — Personalized Baseline Behavior Modeling
We will learn individual-specific behavioral fingerprints from multimodal inputs:
• Textual signals:User journals, chat logs, and free-form writing — encoded via a fine-
tuned transformer (e.g., MentalBERT or a quantized LLaMA variant) to capture linguistic
complexity, vocabulary richness, and semantic coherence.
• Interaction signals:Typing dynamics (keystroke timings, edit rates), session duration,
and app-usage patterns captured through a lightweight client SDK.
• Optional signals:Speech prosody features (speaking rate, pitch variation) and wearable
data (HRV, sleep cycles) if available.
• Baseline profiles will be updated incrementally using an exponential moving average combined
with an online anomaly threshold recalibrator.
4.2 Module 2 — Temporal Mental State Modeling
Rather than discrete labels, we model mental state as acontinuous trajectoryevolving over
time:
• ATemporal Fusion Transformer (TFT)or lightweightLSTM-based encoderwill
embed longitudinal feature sequences into a latent mental-state space.
• The latent representation tracks gradual drift (slow mood decline) as well as sudden anomalies
(acute stress spikes).
• Sliding-window context windows (e.g., 7-day and 30-day) will capture both short-term
fluctuations and longer-term trend shifts.
4.3 Module 3 — Deviation & Anomaly Detection
Personalized thresholds (not population norms) will be used to flag early warning signals:
• Linguistic anomalies:Drops in sentence coherence, increased repetition, reduced vocabu-
lary diversity.
• Behavioral anomalies:Irregular session timings, sudden drop in engagement, erratic
typing patterns.

4.4 Module 4 — Predictive Risk Modeling
Proactive forecasting of upcoming risk states (7-day horizon):
•Target risks:Stress escalation, burnout onset, depressive tendency, social withdrawal.
• Model:Multi-output probabilistic forecaster (TFT or N-BEATS with uncertainty quantifi-
cation via Monte Carlo Dropout).
• Outputs are risk probability distributions, not binary flags, giving users and clinicians
actionable confidence levels.
4.5 Module 5 — Explainability & Feedback Dashboard
Trust and transparency are core requirements:
•SHAP valueson text features will highlight which linguistic markers drove a risk flag.
• Attention-weight visualizationsfrom the temporal model will show which time points
were most influential.
• Natural-language summaries (generated by a grounded LLM module) will translate numeric
signals into plain English, e.g.,"Significant drop in writing coherence detected over the past 5
days.".
• Aclinician-facing viewand auser-facing viewwill be provided with appropriate levels
of detail.

6. Ethical Considerations & Privacy
• Data minimization:Only signals necessary for baseline modeling are collected; raw text is
never stored beyond the session unless explicitly consented to.
• Local-first inference:All model inference runs locally on the user’s device by default,
ensuring no sensitive data leaves the device.
• Consent & control:Users can view, export, or delete their entire behavioral profile at any
time.
• Not a clinical tool:The system will prominently communicate that it is a monitoring aid
and not a substitute for professional mental health care.
• Bias auditing:Baseline models will be evaluated across demographic subgroups to detect
and mitigate systematic bias in risk scoring.
